What implications does the lack of electricity have?

Education

In Sub-Saharan Africa, the lack of access to electricity significantly impacts educational outcomes. Less than two-thirds of children complete primary school , and a staggering 90% of students attend schools without electricity. This limitation restricts the availability of good lighting, which means classes can only be held during daylight hours. Reliable electricity would allow for extended learning times, providing the opportunity for classes to start earlier and end later, thus enhancing the quality of education. This would give students more time to engage with their studies and participate in extracurricular activities. Electricity enables the use of digital tools and resources, such as computers and the internet, which are vital for a modern education. These tools provide access to a vast array of information and educational materials that can enhance learning. Students can benefit from up-to-date textbooks, interactive learning modules, and educational videos, which are often not available in traditional, non-electrified classrooms. Better education is widely recognized as a crucial factor in reducing poverty which leads to better living standars for the people of Sub-Saharan Africa.

Health

The absence of electricity poses severe health risks, particularly for children. Every year, nearly 80,000 young children unintentionally ingest kerosene from lamps, resulting in hospitalization and the need for medical treatment. Alarmingly, 60% of these cases lead to chemically induced pneumonia, a preventable condition if safer lighting options were available through access to electricity.

Additionally, the use of kerosene lamps and traditional biomass for cooking exposes families to harmful indoor air pollution. This pollution is a leading cause of respiratory infections, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and lung cancer. Women and children, who spend more time indoors, are especially vulnerable to these health risks.

Without electricity, healthcare facilities struggle to provide essential services. Many clinics lack refrigeration for vaccines and medicines, reliable lighting for night-time emergencies, and the power needed for essential medical equipment. This hampers the delivery of quality healthcare and increases the risk of disease outbreaks and poor health outcomes.

Closing the Energy Gap in Africa

Africa, with its vast solar resources, receives an average of 2000 kWh of solar radiance annually, making it an ideal location for solar energy production. Here's why solar farms are a promising renewable energy solution for Africa:

  1. Abundant Energy Source: Solar farms harness the sun's energy, which is plentiful and sustainable.
  2. Environmentally Friendly: They produce no emissions or pollution during operation, making them a green energy choice.
  3. Scalability: Solar farms can be easily scaled, allowing for gradual expansion from small to large installations.
  4. Low Maintenance: They require minimal upkeep and operate silently, adding to their practicality.
  5. Land Availability: While solar farms need significant land, this is less of an issue in Africa. In 2022, the population density in Sub-Saharan Africa was approximately 48.3 inhabitants per square kilometer, indicating ample space for solar installations.

Investing in solar power can significantly reduce the energy gap between Africa and other continents, leveraging the continent's natural advantages to create a sustainable and prosperous future. What would it look like if we utilize this untapped energy?
